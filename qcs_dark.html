Source Mode Left Panel ▼

Controls the photon source model used in the simulation.

Ideal — A perfect single-photon emitter. Every pulse produces exactly one photon with no extraneous noise sources. In this mode, QBER is determined solely by the Channel Noise slider. Useful for studying fundamental BB84 mechanics without experimental imperfections.

Realistic — Models a weak coherent pulse (WCP) laser, the most common practical QKD source. This activates an additional QBER penalty of ~8% × Multi-Photon Probability and a ~0.5% dark-count floor. Multi-photon pulses expose the system to Photon-Number-Splitting (PNS) attacks where Eve can split off a photon undetected.

The number of photons Alice's source emits per second (ph/s).

Min1 ph/s
Default50 ph/s
Max100 ph/s
Higher rates increase raw photon throughput and the density of the animated quantum channel.
In practice, emission rate affects the clock speed of the QKD session and the achievable raw key rate, but does not directly affect QBER or security.
Very high rates can exceed detector bandwidth, causing saturation — modeled here by the Detector Efficiency slider.

Channel Noise Level Left Panel ▼

The depolarizing probability of the quantum channel — the most critical parameter in the simulation.

Min0%
Default5%
Max50%
⚠ Threshold11%

Physically models fiber birefringence, thermal photon absorption, polarization misalignment, and detector dark counts. Implemented as a depolarizing channel — with probability p, a Pauli-X gate (bit flip) is applied to the qubit state.

The QBER contribution from noise is approximately equal to this value. Once QBER ≥ 11% (Shor-Preskill bound), the secure key rate drops to zero and the protocol must abort — Eve could have intercepted too many bits.

0–11%: Secure zone — positive secure key rate, channel usable.
>11%: Abort zone — QBER chart turns red, key generation halted.

Detector Efficiency Left Panel ▼

The probability that Bob's single-photon detector successfully registers an incoming photon.

Min10%
Default85%
Max100%

Modeled as a Bernoulli trial: each sifted photon is accepted with probability η (detector efficiency). Photons not registered are discarded.

Detector efficiency reduces the sifted key rate (fewer usable bits) but does not itself introduce bit errors — Bob only records bits when he detects a photon.
Real InGaAs SPDs (infrared) typically achieve 10–30%. Superconducting nanowire SPDs (SNSPDs) can exceed 95%.
Very low efficiency may make key generation impractical even with low noise.
💥
Multi-Photon Probability Left Panel ▼

The probability that a laser pulse contains 2 or more photons. Only active in Realistic source mode.

Min0%
Default10%
Max50%

Multi-photon pulses are vulnerable to Photon-Number-Splitting (PNS) attacks: Eve can block one photon to keep for later measurement while forwarding the other to Bob. This is undetectable because Bob still receives a photon.

The effective QBER increase from multi-photon events is approximately ~8% × mp. Decoy-state protocols were designed specifically to detect and bound PNS attacks. This parameter is grayed out in Ideal mode.

Polarization Basis Left Panel ▼

Selects the display basis for photon state symbols in the visualization and photon state grid.

Rectilinear ↕↔ (Z-basis) — Uses vertical |0⟩ = ↕ and horizontal |1⟩ = ↔ polarization. This is Alice's computational basis where |0⟩ = [1,0] and |1⟩ = [0,1] as quantum state vectors.

Diagonal ↗↘ (X-basis) — Uses +45° |+⟩ = ↗ and −45° |−⟩ = ↘ polarization, where |+⟩ = (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2 and |−⟩ = (|0⟩−|1⟩)/√2.

In BB84, Alice and Bob each independently choose a random basis for every photon. Only pulses where both chose the same basis are kept during sifting (~50% of all pulses). The basis choice itself is never secret — only the measurement outcomes matter.

QBER vs Time Chart Left Panel ▼

Live chart of the Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER) — the fraction of sifted key bits where Alice and Bob's records disagree.

The solid line shows instantaneous QBER with small shot-noise fluctuations to model finite-key effects. The orange dashed line marks the 11% Shor-Preskill security threshold.

Blue line: QBER below threshold — secure key possible.
Red line: QBER above threshold — protocol should abort.

Formula: QBER = errors / sifted_bits. With Eve active, QBER ≈ p + 0.25(1−p) where p is channel noise, because Eve's basis mismatch (~50% of intercepts) each contribute a 50% error probability.

👁
Eavesdropper (Eve) Toggle Center Bar ▼

Enables or disables an intercept-resend attack by Eve — the eavesdropper positioned midway on the quantum channel.

When Enabled, for every photon Alice sends, Eve intercepts it and measures it in a randomly chosen basis (Z or X) using the Born rule. She then re-prepares and forwards a new photon in her measured state to Bob. Because the No-Cloning Theorem prevents copying a quantum state, this is the best she can do.

Physics outcome: when Eve's basis matches Alice's (~50% of cases), she learns the bit with no disturbance. When they mismatch (~50%), the qubit collapses to a random eigenstate in Eve's basis, leaving Bob with a ~50% chance of a wrong bit. Net effect: +25% QBER.

Eve's channel-interception glow and node appear in the visualization.
Eve's partial key and corrupted message attempt appear in the right panel.
Detection probability P = 1 − (3/4)^K where K is the number of bits checked. With 200 check bits, detection is effectively certain (≈99.999%).
🔐
Privacy Amplification & Key Rate Right Panel ▼

After sifting, Privacy Amplification (PA) compresses the raw sifted key to remove any information Eve may have gained, using the Shor-Preskill bound.

Secure key rate: r = max(0, 1 − 2h₂(QBER)), where h₂ is the binary Shannon entropy function. The final secure key has ⌊rawSifted × r⌋ bits.

PA uses universal hashing (Toeplitz matrices in practice) — here the length reduction is simulated. The OTP (One-Time Pad) requirement demands at least 8 × messageLength secure key bits. Key bits are never reused.

r → 1 at QBER = 0%: no information leaked, full key usable.
r → 0 at QBER ≈ 14.6% (exact Shor-Preskill zero). The practical 11% threshold gives margin for reconciliation overhead.
Below zero: protocol aborts, no key generated.
